# IMHWF 2026 Reframed Concept Paper

## Proposed Title

**MindBridge Global: A Human-Centered Digital Ecosystem for Continuous, Community-Connected Mental Health Care**

## Optional Subtitle

**From a low-cost local prototype to a scalable WHO-aligned model for triage, multidisciplinary coordination, continuity of care, and privacy-preserving mental health intelligence**

## Executive Summary

Mental health systems around the world are under pressure from rising demand, workforce shortages, fragmented services, repeated assessments, weak continuity after discharge, and limited use of routine service data for prevention and quality improvement. These challenges are shared across high-, middle-, and low-resource settings, even though infrastructure and staffing levels differ.

This project proposes **MindBridge Global**, a human-centered digital mental health ecosystem designed to improve continuity, coordination, and equity across the full care journey. It combines structured pre-session check-in, professional risk review, multidisciplinary care planning, discharge and community follow-up, and privacy-preserving data systems for service improvement and research. The model is built on a principle that is both future-facing and safe: **technology should reduce avoidable workload and improve timely support, while human judgment, rights, dignity, and community inclusion remain central**.

What makes this proposal especially competitive is that it does not present innovation as "AI replacing professionals." Instead, it presents a realistic and scalable model for **AI-supported, human-supervised mental health service transformation**. It starts from an already practical Google Apps Script prototype for multidisciplinary workflow coordination, then shows a credible pathway toward a more secure, interoperable, ethical, and internationally adaptable platform.

The concept directly aligns with major global priorities:

- WHO Comprehensive Mental Health Action Plan 2013-2030
- WHO World Mental Health Report: *Transforming mental health for all*
- SDG 3.4 on promoting mental health and well-being
- SDG 3.8 on universal health coverage
- SDG 3.c on strengthening the health workforce
- SDG 17.16 and 17.18 on partnerships and high-quality data

In competition terms, the project is strong because it is **innovative, feasible, equity-oriented, measurable, and scalable across countries**.

## Reframed Core Idea

The strongest way to position this project is not as a single hospital tool and not as a single-discipline workflow. It should be presented as:

**A scalable mental health continuity platform that links early identification, multidisciplinary teamwork, community-based follow-up, and learning health systems.**

This framing makes the project feel:

- more global
- more future-ready
- more aligned with WHO goals
- more attractive to judges looking for systems impact

## Global Problem Statement

Across countries, mental health care is too often organized in disconnected steps rather than continuous support journeys. Patients and families repeat the same story to different professionals. High-risk psychosocial issues may be missed. Community follow-up is inconsistent. Workforce time is consumed by repetitive data collection instead of direct care. At the same time, services often lack structured, ethically governed data that could support prevention, planning, research, and policy.

These gaps matter because mental health outcomes are shaped not only by symptoms, but also by housing, income insecurity, caregiving burden, stigma, family conflict, violence, employment, and access barriers. A modern mental health system therefore needs more than medical records alone. It needs a **coordinated human-and-digital ecosystem** that supports clinical care, psychosocial care, and community continuity together.

## Proposed Innovation

MindBridge Global is a phased digital ecosystem with five connected functions:

### 1. Smart front-door access

Patients, caregivers, or staff complete a structured check-in before or at contact with services. This captures presenting concerns, psychosocial barriers, follow-up needs, and urgent warning signs early.

### 2. Human-supervised decision support

The Phase 1 prototype uses rule-based flags for professional review. In future phases, validated AI-assisted screening can help identify patterns linked to suicide risk, relapse, caregiver strain, violence, housing insecurity, treatment interruption, or service disengagement. Final decisions always remain with trained professionals.

### 3. Shared multidisciplinary coordination

Psychiatrists, nurses, psychologists, social workers, occupational therapists, and community teams can view role-relevant information, reduce duplicated assessments, coordinate plans, and track responsibilities across the care pathway.

### 4. Seamless discharge-to-community continuity

The platform supports referral tracking, follow-up reminders, care transitions, and coordination with primary care, community mental health teams, NGOs, peer networks, and social services within clearly defined consent boundaries.

### 5. Privacy-preserving learning and policy intelligence

De-identified or pseudonymized data can be used for dashboards, quality improvement, service redesign, workforce planning, and future cross-site learning without exposing unnecessary personal details.

This reshaped version is stronger than a standard digital tool proposal because it tells judges that the innovation is not just software. The innovation is the **integration of care, community, ethics, and evidence**.

## Local Prototype to Global Platform

The proposal should clearly show a staged path:

### Phase 1. Operational prototype

Low-cost workflow model using Google Apps Script, Google Sheets, and web forms to test real service operations, staff acceptance, data elements, and patient flow.

### Phase 2. Standardized mental health data model

Create a common data dictionary covering symptoms, psychosocial determinants, risk factors, follow-up status, referral outcomes, and service barriers.

### Phase 3. Secure care coordination layer

Add consent management, role-based access control, audit trails, secure hosting, and clearer separation of clinical, psychosocial, and public coordination views.

### Phase 4. Responsible AI support

Introduce validated AI-assisted screening only after local ethics review, bias monitoring, safety evaluation, and clear escalation protocols.

### Phase 5. Multi-site and cross-country learning network

Use federated or privacy-preserving data approaches to compare service patterns, strengthen workforce planning, and generate practice-based evidence across settings.

This roadmap gives the proposal maturity. It says: we are not overpromising; we are building toward a realistic global model.

## Equity and Global Relevance

One reason this concept can stand out internationally is that it is relevant beyond a single country. It is especially valuable for settings where:

- specialist mental health staff are limited
- patients travel long distances for care
- psychosocial needs are high
- paper systems or disconnected digital systems slow coordination
- community follow-up is inconsistent
- research-quality data are hard to generate from routine services

The model is attractive because it can begin with affordable tools in resource-limited settings and evolve toward stronger infrastructure where capacity exists. That gives it true global relevance instead of high-tech exclusivity.

## Ethics, Safety, and Rights-Based Design

This section should stay very strong because judges will care about safety.

Key safeguards:

- informed consent and transparent data use
- minimum necessary access by role
- human oversight for every high-risk alert
- no automated clinical decision-making
- de-identification for research and analytics where possible
- audit trails for access and editing
- bias review before deploying AI-assisted screening
- crisis escalation pathways linked to real clinical teams
- compliance with local privacy laws and health data regulations

The ethical message should be simple:

**This is not surveillance technology. This is rights-based continuity-of-care infrastructure.**
